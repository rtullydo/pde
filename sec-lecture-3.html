    Section 1.3 Lecture 3 - Three fundamental equations

    Subsection 1.3.1 Diffusion

    Imagine a drop of food coloring placed in a glass of water. The dye spreads out, or diffuses into the liquid. The diffusion equation describes how this happens. Many physical processes can be modeled by diffusion, probably most notably the transfer of heat in a medium.
    We will derive a simple diffusion equation in one variable. Imagine a thin tube arranged along the \(x\)-axis filled with a static liquid. Let \(u(x,t)\) be the mass concentration of dye in the tube at position \(x\) at time \(t\text{.}\) A simple physical observation is that the dye moves in the liquid at a rate proportional to the concetration gradient at a given point. Fix a small segment of the tube between two positions \(x_0, x_1\text{.}\) The mass of dye in the small segment is
    \begin{equation*} M(t) = \int_{x_0}^{x_1} u(x,t) \, dx. \end{equation*}
    This gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dM}{dt} = \int_{x_0}^{x_1} u_t(x, t) \, dx. \end{equation*}
    On the other hand,
    \begin{align*} \frac{dM}{dt} \amp = \text{flow in - flow out} \\ \amp = k u_x(x_1, t) - k u_x(x_0, t) \end{align*}
    as flow is proportional to the contentration gradient.
    Equating these expressions for \(dM/dt\text{,}\) we see
    \begin{equation*} \int_{x_0}^{x_1} u_t(x, t) \, dx = k u_x(x_1, t) - k u_x(x_0). \end{equation*}
    Now take the derivative of this expression with respect to \(x_1\) and apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, which gives
    \begin{equation*} u_t(x_1, t) = k u_{x x_1}(x_1, t) \end{equation*}
    and make the observation that this is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} u_t = k u_{xx}. \end{equation*}

    Simple diffusion in 1D.

    The simple diffusion equation or heat equation on the line is
    \begin{equation*} u_t = k u_{xx}. \end{equation*}
    Now, let’s do it again, but this time in 3D. Instead of a small segment of a tube, imagine a small sphere in 3D space, with the interior ball denoted \(D\) and the boundary sphere \(\partial D\text{.}\) The mass concentration of the dye is given by \(u(x, y, z, t)\) at position \(x, y, z\) and time \(t\text{.}\) The mass of dye inside \(D\) is
    \begin{equation*} M(t) = \iiint_D u(x, y, z, t)\, dV, \end{equation*}
    and as before
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dM}{dt} = \iiint_D u_t \, dV. \end{equation*}
    As in the 1D case, we’ll make the simplfying assumption that the movement of the dye inside \(D\) is proportional to the concentration gradient with constant \(k\text{,}\) but this time we need to account for all of the boundary points in \(\partial D\text{.}\) At each point in \(\partial D\text{,}\) choose an outward normal vector \(n\text{.}\) Then the flux of dye through the surface locally at a point \((x, y, z)\in \partial D\) is proportional to the outward concentration gradient through the surface area unit \(dS\text{,}\) which we can write as the expression \(k (n \cdot \nabla u) \, dS\text{.}\) To add up the net change in mass, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dM}{dt} = \iint_{\partial D} k(n \cdot \nabla u)\, dS. \end{equation*}
    As before, we set the two expressions for \(dM/dt\) equal to get
    \begin{equation*} \iiint_D u_t \, dV = \iint_{\partial D} k(n \cdot \nabla u)\, dS. \end{equation*}
    Now apply the divergence theorem.

    Divergence theorem.

    Given a solid region \(D\) with closed boundary surface \(\partial D\text{,}\) an outward normal \(n\) to \(\partial D\) and a \(C^1\) function \(F\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \iiint_{D} \nabla \cdot F \, dV = \iint_{\partial D} (F \cdot n) \, dS. \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} \iiint_D u_t \, dV \amp = \iint_{\partial D} k(n \cdot \nabla u)\, dS \\ \amp = \iiint_D \nabla \cdot k\nabla u \, dV\\ \amp = \iiint_D k \nabla \cdot \nabla u \, dV \\ \amp = \iiint_D k (u_{xx} + u_{yy} + u_{zz})\, dV \end{align*}
    Then we conclude (invoking A LOT of high level math) that since
    \begin{equation*} \iiint_D u_t \, dV = \iiint_D k (u_{xx} + u_{yy} + u_{zz})\, dV \end{equation*}
    for any small ball \(D\) that
    \begin{equation*} u_t = k (u_{xx} + u_{yy} + u_{zz}) = k \Delta u. \end{equation*}

    Simple diffusion in 3D.

    The simple diffusion equation in space or 3D heat equation is
    \begin{equation*} u_t = k \Delta u. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.3.2 The wave equation

    1D Wave equation.

    For \(u(x,t)\text{,}\) the 1D wave equation is
    \begin{equation*} u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}. \end{equation*}

    3D Wave equation.

    For \(u(x, y, z, t)\text{,}\) the 3D wave equation is
    \begin{equation*} u_{tt} = c^2 \Delta u. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.3.3 Laplace’s equation

    In any of the previous equations we have that the physical scenario does not change with respect to time, we get the reduction
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \Delta u. \end{equation*}
    The is called Laplace’s equation and the solutions to the equation are called harmonic functions.
